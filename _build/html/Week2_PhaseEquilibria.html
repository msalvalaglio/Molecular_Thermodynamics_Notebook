Topic 1: Liquid/Vapour Equilibrium conditions: deriving the Raoult Equation#

To express the partial pressure of component \(i\) in a vapour phase in equilibrium with a multicomponent liquid phase we shall begin by expressing the fundamental condition that has to be fulfilled by all species at thermodynamic equilibrium at constant \(T\) and \(P\):

(1)#\[ \mu_i^L(T,P,\vec{x})=\mu_i^V(T,P,\vec{y}) \]

where \(\mu_i^L(T,P,\vec{x})\) and \(\mu_i^V(T,P,\vec{y})\) are the chemical potentials for specie \(i\) in the liquid and vapour phase, respectively.

For mixtures of ideal gases the chemical potential is conveniently expressed in differential form as a function of the partial pressure of component \(i\), \(p_i\) as \(d\mu_i=RTd\ln{p_i}\).
In analogy with this expression in the case of real gases fugacity \(f_i\) is introduced, leading to the differential expression \(d\mu_i=RTd\ln{f_i}\). Integrating this expression between the phases in equilibrium yields:

\[ \int_{L,T,P,\vec{x}}^{V,T,P,\vec{y}}d\mu_i=\int_{L,T,P,\vec{x}}^{V,T,P,\vec{y}}RTd\ln{f_i} \]
(2)#\[ \mu_i^V(T,P,\vec{y})-\mu_i^L(T,P,\vec{x})=RT\ln\frac{f_i^V(T,P,\vec{y}}{f_i^L(T,P,\vec{x})} \]

Hence, the equilibrium condition expressed by Eq. (1) can be conveniently stated as:

(3)#\[ f_i^V(T,P,\vec{y})=f_i^L(T,P,\vec{x}) \]

The left-hand term can be written as:

(4)#\[ f_i^V(T,P,\vec{y})=\phi(T,P,\vec{y})Py_i \]

where \(\phi(T,P,\vec{y})\) is the fugacity coefficient, \(P\) the pressure and \(y_i\) the molar fraction in the vapour phase.

The right hand side term can instead be written as:

(5)#\[ f_i^L(T,P,\vec{x})=\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x})x_i\phi(T,P^o(T))P^o(T)e^{\frac{v_i(P-P^o(T))}{RT}} \]

where \(x_i\) is the molar fraction of component \(i\) in the liquid phase, \(\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x})\) is the activity coefficient for component i in the liquid mixture, \(P^o(T)\) is the equilibrium vapour pressure of the pure component \(i\), \(\phi(T,P^o(T))\) is the fugacity coefficient for the pure component \(i\) at \(T\) and \(P^o(T)\), and the term \(e^{\frac{v_i(P-P^o(T))}{RT}}\) is the Poynting correction. For a discussion on the origin of the Poynting correction please refer to the notes on osmosis.

Introducing Eq.(4) and Eq. (5) in Eq.(3), while considering negligible the Poynting correction, and ideal gas approximation applicable yields:

(6)#\[ Py_i=\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x})P^o(T)x_i \]

It should be noted that introducing the further hypothesis of ideal liquid mixture yields the Raoult law:

(7)#\[ Py_i=P^o(T)x_i \]

Topic 2: Liquid/Vapour Equilibrium conditions: deriving the Raoult Equation#

Let us begin by writing the solid-liquid equilibrium conditions at temperature \(T\) and pressure \(P\), between a pure crystalline phase and a multicomponent liquid phase characterised by composition \(\vec{x}\). Equilibrium conditions is stated by the equality of chemical potentials:

(8)#\[ \mu_i^s(T,P)=\mu_i^\ell(T,P,\vec{x}) \]

which is equivalent to the equality of the fugacity in the two phases:

(9)#\[ f_i^s(T,P)=f_i^\ell(T,P,\vec{x}) \]

Focusing on the right hand of Eq.(9), the fugacity in the liquid phase can be rewritten as a function of the fugacity of the pure liquid phase as follows:

(10)#\[ f_i^\ell(T,P,\vec{x})=f_i^\ell(T,P)x_i\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x}) \]

where \(x_i\) is the molar fraction of the solute and \(\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x})\) is the activity coefficient of that specie in the liquid phase.

Let us now focus on the left hand side of Eq. (9). The fugacity of the pure solid phase can also be written as a function of the fugacity of the pure liquid:

(11)#\[ \int^{T,P,s}_{T,P,\ell}d\mu_i=\int^{T,P,s}_{T,P,\ell}RTd\ln{f_i} \]
(12)#\[ \Delta{\mu}_{i,\ell\rightarrow{s}}=RTln\left(\frac{f_i^s(T,P)}{f_i^\ell(T,P)}\right) \]
(13)#\[ f_i^s(T,P)=f_i^\ell(T,P)e^{\frac{\Delta{\mu}_{i,\ell\rightarrow{s}}}{RT}} \]

The change in chemical potential can be written for a pure substance as the partial molar change in free energy \(\Delta{g_i(T,P)}\):

(14)#\[ \Delta\mu_{i,\ell\rightarrow{s}}=\Delta{g}_i^s(T,P)=\Delta{h}_i(T,P)-T\Delta{s}_i(T,P) \]

The molar enthalpy change is:

(15)#\[ \Delta{h}_i(T,P)=-\Delta{h_{fus}\left(T_f\right)}+\int_T^{T_f}Cp^{\ell}dT+\int_T^{T_f}Cp^{s}dT\simeq-\Delta{h_{fus}\left(T_f\right)}+\left(Cp^{\ell}-Cp^{s}\right)\left(T_f-T\right) \]

while the molar entropy change is written as:

(16)#\[ \Delta{s}_i(T,P)=-\frac{\Delta{h_{fus}\left(T_f\right)}}{T_f}+\int_T^{T_f}\frac{Cp^{\ell}}{T}dT+\int_T^{T_f}\frac{Cp^{s}}{T}dT \simeq -\frac{\Delta{h_{fus}}}{T_f}+\left(Cp^{\ell}-Cp^{s}\right)\ln{\left(\frac{T_f}{T}\right)} \]

Defining \(\Delta{Cp}_{\ell\rightarrow{s}}=\left(Cp^{\ell}-Cp^{s}\right)\)

(17)#\[ \Delta{g}_i^s(T,P)=-\Delta{h_{fus}\left(1-\frac{T}{T_f}\right)}+\Delta{Cp}_{\ell\rightarrow{s}}\left(T_f-T-T\ln{\left(\frac{T_f}{T}\right)}\right) \]

Since \(\Delta{Cp}_{\ell\rightarrow{s}}\) is usually small compared the above expression is typically simplified to:

(18)#\[ \Delta{g}_i^s(T,P)\simeq-\Delta{h_{fus}\left(1-\frac{T}{T_f}\right)} \]

Inserting this equation into the equality of fugacities yields:

(19)#\[ f_i^\ell(T,P)x_i\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x})=f_i^\ell(T,P)e^{\frac{\Delta{\mu}_{i,\ell\rightarrow{s}}}{RT}} \]

hence the solubility of component \(i\), \(x_i\) is:

(20)#\[ x_i=\frac{1}{\gamma_i(T,P,\vec{x})}e^{-\frac{\Delta{h_{fus}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T}-\frac{1}{T_f}\right)} \]
